Finance at Large

The financial industry is not monolithic. It is a collection of distinct sectors — each with a different primary function, customer, revenue model, and regulatory framework.

Finance at large · Banking

Banking

The core financial intermediary — connecting savers to borrowers
Retail & commercial banks

Deposits & lending

Accept deposits, extend loans (mortgages, auto, commercial), provide checking/savings accounts and payment services. Revenue model: net interest margin (spread between deposit rates and loan rates).

Examples: JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, Regions, Truist

Investment banks

Capital markets & advisory

Help corporations and governments raise capital (IPOs, debt issuance), advise on M&A transactions, and facilitate large securities trades. Do not take retail deposits. Revenue: fees and trading spreads.

Examples: Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan (IB division), Lazard

Universal banks

Both under one roof

Large institutions that combine retail banking, commercial lending, investment banking, and wealth management. The dominant U.S. model post-Gramm-Leach-Bliley (1999), which repealed Glass-Steagall separation.

Examples: JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Citigroup

Finance at large · Asset management

Asset Management

Firms that invest capital on behalf of others — in exchange for a management fee
Traditional / long-only managers

Mutual funds & ETFs

Manage pooled investment vehicles — active mutual funds or passive index strategies — distributed to retail and institutional investors. Revenue: expense ratios (basis points on AUM). Fee compression has been dramatic over the past 20 years.

Examples: Vanguard, Fidelity, BlackRock, T. Rowe Price, American Funds

Alternative asset managers

Hedge funds, private equity, venture capital

Manage concentrated, often illiquid strategies for institutional investors and accredited individuals. Typical fee structure: "2 and 20" (2% management fee + 20% of profits). Increasingly democratized through interval funds and private credit vehicles.

Examples: Blackstone, KKR, Citadel, Sequoia, Bridgewater

Institutional asset managers

Pension, endowment & sovereign wealth

Manage large pools of capital for specific institutional mandates — public pensions, corporate pensions, university endowments, foundations, and sovereign wealth funds. Custom mandates, long time horizons, liability-driven investing.

Examples: PIMCO, Wellington, State Street Global Advisors

Key distinction from advisory

Asset managers don't advise — they manage

Asset managers run the portfolios that advisors put clients in. They are the product manufacturers. Financial advisors select from asset managers' products on behalf of clients — the two roles are distinct, though some firms do both.

Finance at large · Insurance

Insurance Companies

Risk transfer businesses that also function as major capital allocators
Life & health insurers

Protection & income

Provide life insurance, disability insurance, long-term care coverage, and annuities. Annuities blur the line between insurance and investment — they are a major product in the wealth management space.

Examples: Northwestern Mutual, MassMutual, New York Life, Prudential

P&C insurers

Property & casualty

Cover losses from accidents, natural disasters, liability events. Less relevant to wealth management directly, but relevant to HNW planning (umbrella policies, private client insurance for art, jewelry, property).

Examples: State Farm, Allstate, Chubb, AIG

Role in wealth management

Distribution and product

Life insurance companies are major product manufacturers for wealth advisors — annuities, life insurance, and disability products flow through captive agents or independent channels. Many life insurers also own or affiliate with broker-dealers.

Finance at large · Market infrastructure

Exchanges, Custodians & Market Infrastructure

The plumbing that makes markets and advice delivery possible
Exchanges & trading venues

Where securities are bought and sold

Stock exchanges (NYSE, Nasdaq) and alternative trading systems (dark pools, ECNs) provide the marketplace for securities transactions. Revenue: listing fees, transaction fees, data fees. Increasingly dominated by electronic market makers.

Clearing & settlement

The post-trade layer

Clearinghouses (DTCC, Options Clearing Corp) guarantee trades and manage counterparty risk. Settlement (T+1 in the U.S. as of 2024) is the process of actually transferring cash and securities after a trade.

Custodians

Critical to the advisory business

Custodians hold client assets on behalf of RIAs and IBDs — they are the safekeeping layer. Advisors use custodian platforms for trading, reporting, and account management. Not the same as the advisor's firm.

Major custodians: Charles Schwab, Fidelity Institutional, Pershing (BNY Mellon)

Fintech

Disrupting every layer

Fintechs operate across banking (Chime, SoFi), lending (LendingClub), payments (Stripe, PayPal), robo-advice (Betterment, Wealthfront), and financial planning software (eMoney, MoneyGuidePro). Often B2B infrastructure for traditional advisors.

02

The Advisory Channel

Within the broader financial industry, the advisory channel is the segment focused on delivering personalized financial guidance to individuals, families, and institutions.

Advisory channel · 01 of 04

Wirehouses

The traditional full-service brokerage model
Examples

The "Big Four"

  • Merrill Lynch (Bank of America)
  • Morgan Stanley Wealth Management
  • UBS Wealth Management
  • Wells Fargo Advisors
Structure

Employee model

Advisors are W-2 employees. They operate under the firm's BD license and use proprietary platforms, research, and products. The firm controls the client relationship legally.

Economics

Grid-based payout

Advisors typically receive 35–45% of production. Firms retain the rest for overhead, compliance, and profit. Grid-based structures incentivize revenue growth.

Client profile

Full spectrum, HNW-focused

Wirehouses serve all segments but have invested heavily in private banking and family office capabilities for HNW/UHNW clients.

Advisory channel · 02 of 04

Independent RIAs

The fastest-growing channel in the industry
Definition

Registered Investment Advisers

Registered directly with the SEC (AUM ≥ $100M) or state regulators. No affiliation to a broker-dealer or product manufacturer. Full fiduciary obligation to clients.

Examples

Wide range of sizes

  • Solo practitioners ($50M–$250M AUM)
  • Ensemble firms ($250M–$2B)
  • Large RIAs: Fisher, Creative Planning, Edelman, Capital Investment Advisors
Fiduciary duty

Legally required to act in client's best interest

Unlike BDs, RIAs cannot recommend a product simply because it's "suitable." They owe clients an ongoing duty of loyalty and care.

Custodians

Third-party custody

RIAs use independent custodians — Schwab, Fidelity, Pershing — rather than holding client assets directly, providing an important safety layer.

Advisory channel · 03 of 04

Independent Broker-Dealers (IBDs)

The middle ground between wirehouse and pure independence
Examples

Major IBDs

  • LPL Financial (largest in the U.S.)
  • Raymond James Financial Services
  • Ameriprise Financial
  • Commonwealth Financial Network
Structure

Contractor model

Advisors are independent contractors (1099) who affiliate with the IBD to use its BD license, compliance infrastructure, and technology platform.

Economics

Higher payout, more overhead

Advisors typically retain 80–95% of production but pay their own overhead (rent, staff, technology). Greater autonomy, less firm support.

Product shelf

Open architecture

IBD advisors have access to a broader product shelf than wirehouse advisors, though the IBD may still have preferred vendor agreements.

Advisory channel · 04 of 04

Banks & Insurance-Based Channels

Captive distribution tied to a parent institution
Bank-based advisors

Branch referral model

Advisors placed inside retail bank branches. Referrals come from tellers and bankers. Heavy focus on managed accounts, annuities, and simple planning for mass-market clients.

Insurance-based

Captive agents

Agents (Northwestern Mutual, New York Life, MassMutual) are primarily trained on insurance and annuity products. Many pursue CFP® credentials to expand into broader planning.

Limitations

Narrow product shelf

Captive advisors typically sell only proprietary or approved products, limiting objectivity. Conflicts must be disclosed but are structural to the model.

Strengths

Distribution at scale

Many Americans get their first financial guidance through a bank or insurance channel. Strong for protection-based planning (life, disability, long-term care).

Regulatory structure

Dually Registered Advisors

Common at wirehouses and large IBDs — and a source of frequent client confusion
What it means

Wearing two hats

An advisor is registered as both a registered representative (BD) and an investment adviser representative (IAR). They can act in either capacity depending on the account type.

Why it matters

The standard shifts by account

In a brokerage account, they owe suitability. In an advisory account, they owe fiduciary duty. Clients may not realize which standard applies to which recommendation.

Disclosure

Form CRS

Reg BI (effective 2020) requires broker-dealers to provide a Client Relationship Summary (Form CRS) disclosing registration type, services offered, and material conflicts.

The right question

"Are you a fiduciary — for all my accounts, all the time?"

A "yes" in one context doesn't guarantee it in another. Clients should ask this explicitly and review the advisor's Form CRS and ADV Part 2.

Compensation models

The Four Compensation Structures

How advisors are paid shapes what they recommend
AUM-based fees

Most common model today

Advisor charges a % of assets under management — typically 0.50%–1.25% annually, tiered by account size. Aligns advisor growth with client growth, but may discourage advice outside the managed account.

Commission-based

Transaction-driven

Advisor earns a commission when a product is sold — mutual fund loads, annuity commissions (4–7%), insurance premiums. Creates potential incentive to trade or sell products more frequently.

Flat fee / retainer

Growing, especially in RIA space

Client pays a fixed annual fee (e.g., $5,000–$25,000/yr) for ongoing planning regardless of asset level. Increasingly popular for younger clients with high income but lower investable assets.

Hourly billing

Least common; most transactional

Client pays an hourly rate ($200–$500/hr) for defined engagements — a second opinion, a retirement income analysis, a specific planning question. No ongoing relationship.

Compensation · Key distinction

"Fee-Only" vs. "Fee-Based"

These sound similar but mean very different things — and the difference matters
Fee-only

No commissions — ever

The advisor and their firm receive compensation only from client-paid fees. They do not accept commissions, trails, 12b-1 fees, or any third-party compensation. Lowest potential for conflicts. NAPFA membership requires fee-only status.

Fee-based

Fees AND commissions

The advisor charges advisory fees on managed accounts but also earns commissions on products sold in brokerage accounts (annuities, insurance, mutual fund loads). Dually registered advisors are almost always fee-based.

Watch out for

Loose use of terminology in marketing

Advisors and firms sometimes use "fee-based" and "fee-only" interchangeably in client-facing materials, even though they are legally and practically distinct. Clients should review Form CRS and Form ADV Part 2 to understand actual compensation arrangements.

Client segments · UHNW

Family Office Structures

The most comprehensive — and complex — wealth management model
Single-family office (SFO)

One family, full staff

A dedicated entity that employs investment, tax, legal, and administrative staff exclusively for one ultra-wealthy family. Typically requires $100M+ to justify $1–3M+ in annual overhead.

Multi-family office (MFO)

Shared SFO economics

Delivers family office-level services to multiple families, spreading overhead. Services include consolidated reporting, alternatives access, estate/trust administration, and philanthropy coordination.

Virtual family office

Outsourced coordination

An RIA or wealth manager acts as "quarterback," coordinating across the client's existing CPA, attorney, and other specialists. The most cost-effective model for the $5–$50M range.

Summary

Key takeaways

1

Finance is not one industry — it's a collection of distinct sectors (banking, asset management, insurance, advisory, infrastructure) with different customers, economics, and regulatory frameworks.

2

Asset managers and insurance companies are product manufacturers. Financial advisors are the distribution channel that selects and places those products with clients.

3

Within the advisory channel, firm type — wirehouse, RIA, IBD, or captive — determines the advisor's product shelf, regulatory obligations, and structural conflicts.

4

Registration type (BD vs. RIA) is the most important regulatory distinction. RIAs are held to a fiduciary standard; BD representatives are held to a suitability standard under Reg BI.

5

"Fee-only" and "fee-based" are not interchangeable. Only fee-only advisors are fully free from commission-based compensation conflicts.

6

Service models scale with wealth. Mass-market clients are served digitally; HNW clients get dedicated advisors; UHNW clients get dedicated teams. Matching model to complexity is the central business challenge.
